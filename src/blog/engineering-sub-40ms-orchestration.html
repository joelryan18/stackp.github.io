Engineering sub-40ms orchestration: reflexes, not workflows

2026-06-107 MIN READBY THE AXON TEAM

Touch a hot stove and your hand moves in about 30 milliseconds. The signal doesn't travel to your brain, get triaged, wait for a planning meeting, and come back with an approved remediation workflow. It's handled at the spinal cord — close to the sensor, close to the muscle. Biology calls this a reflex arc. We think it's the correct architecture for agent orchestration, and we built AXON around it.

The workflow-engine trap

Most orchestration platforms descend from batch pipelines: a central scheduler owns a queue, workers poll it, every hand-off is a round trip through the hub. That shape is fine when the work is "render a report nightly." It collapses when the work is "an incident is unfolding and four agents need to coordinate now." Every hop through a central broker adds serialization, a queue wait, and a network round trip — 150 to 400 ms per hand-off in the systems we measured. Chain five hand-offs and your "autonomous response" is two seconds of bureaucracy before anything happens.

A reflex that takes two seconds is not a reflex. It's a committee with good branding.

What we cut to get under 40ms

Our p99 target for agent-to-agent hand-off is 40 ms, wall-clock, including routing, guardrail checks, and trace write. Four decisions got us there:

  • Hand-offs at the edge. Agents that talk to each other are co-scheduled onto the same edge region. A hand-off is a local call, not a trip through a central hub. The hub only sees the trace, asynchronously.
  • Guardrails as compiled policy. Permission checks run as a pre-compiled decision table at the call site — microseconds — instead of a policy-service round trip. Scope changes recompile and propagate; the hot path never blocks on them.
  • Speculative routing. While an agent is finishing a step, the router has already warmed the most likely next agent — context loaded, connection open. When the plan branch is confirmed, the baton is already in motion. Mispredictions cost us a warm worker; predictions save a cold start.
  • Async everything that isn't the decision. Trace persistence, metrics, memory writes — all off the hot path, all reconciled with backpressure. The only synchronous work is the decision and the act.

Retries and failure at reflex speed

Fast coordination is easy until something breaks. The router treats failure as a first-class routing outcome: a failed step re-routes to a retry with jittered backoff, a slow path triggers a parallel hedge request, and a repeated failure trips a circuit that escalates to a human — with the full trace attached. None of this waits on a central scheduler tick. The p99 for "detect failure → alternative path dispatched" is under 60 ms in production workloads.

Why latency is a trust issue, not a vanity metric

Teams trust automation in proportion to how it behaves mid-incident. An agent swarm that coordinates in milliseconds contains a blast radius while a human is still reading the page alert. One that coordinates in seconds is a spectator with API keys. The uncomfortable truth: by the time most workflow engines finish orchestrating a response, the window where the response mattered has often closed.

Reflexes first. Deliberation where it belongs — in the planner, before the incident, and in the review, after. The moment in between belongs to the spinal cord.
