[ ARENA · 01 ]

Signal Strike

A first-person last-one-standing arena shooter that runs entirely in the browser — drop from the sky onto a neon island, scavenge, fight eleven AI units, and outlast a storm ring that never stops closing. Play solo, or share a four-letter room code and drop with up to three friends in one shared match. One WebGL world, no downloads, no accounts.

Signal Strike takes its match structure from the battle-royale lineage — a glider drop you steer to any district on the island, a purple storm ring that shrinks in timed phases and hurts more each time, shield cells and ammo caches scattered as floor loot, shield siphon on every elimination, and a placement screen when you fall — and its gunplay from the tactical-shooter lineage: every weapon is instant hitscan with a learnable recoil pattern that climbs then weaves, first-shot accuracy that decays while you move or jump, headshots that ring out with a distinct dink, a sprint-slide that carries your momentum under fire, and a hardlight barrier you can throw down to cut a fight in half. The lobby's arsenal panel lets you pick any two of five weapons into your deploy loadout — a full-auto assault rifle, a marksman rifle whose aim-down-sights is a true telescopic scope in the Call of Duty tradition — full lens overlay with reticle hairs and stadia ticks, a hard 5×-class zoom, a quick-scope black blink, breath sway you steady by holding SHIFT, and scope-shadow parallax when you swing fast; scoping snaps you to the glass from either camera, a run-and-gun SMG that barely punishes firing on the move, a ten-pellet close-range shotgun, and a precise three-round-burst rifle — and the choice persists between sessions. You can also fight from a first-person or an over-the-shoulder third-person camera (V swaps them mid-match); every combatant, including you, is an articulated character rig with a real run cycle, crouch, slide, skydive and aim poses. Twelve combatants enter — you and eleven AI units with names like VOLT, HEX and NOVA — and the AI genuinely fights itself: units patrol between districts, trade fire, take storm damage, and eliminate each other whether or not you are involved, so the kill feed and the units-remaining counter tell a real story every match. If you fall, you spectate the unit that dropped you until the match resolves.

The lobby now offers two match modes. Battle royale is the original ruleset described above. Team Deathmatch is a 6v6 mode on a fixed combat ring: two squads — SIGNAL in lime, STATIC in magenta — spawn at opposite bases, every elimination scores a point for the killer's team, and death costs you exactly five seconds before you redeploy at your base with a fresh loadout. First team to thirty eliminations wins, or the higher score when the five-minute clock runs out. Friendly fire is off, teammates stay lime on the minimap, floor loot recycles on a timer, and the AI units filling out both teams genuinely duel each other, so the scoreboard moves even when you don't. In a squad room the host's mode choice rules the match for everyone.

SQUAD LINK is the multiplayer layer: create a room, share the four-letter room code (or the invite link), and up to four humans drop into the same match while AI units fill the remaining slots. Every player's own aim and movement are authoritative on their machine — hits favor the shooter, the way fast online shooters do it — while the earliest joiner hosts the storm clock and the AI, and if the host disconnects mid-match another squad member silently assumes authority. The squad's signals travel over a realtime broadcast relay (the same Supabase project that powers sign-in elsewhere on this domain) — that relay is the one and only thing on this page that touches a server: there are still no accounts, no matchmaking queue and no telemetry, and solo matches never open a connection at all.

Everything is hand-built and procedural: the island — six tinted tower districts with flickering window grids around a central antenna plaza — is generated from one fixed seed so the map is learnable across matches; the entire soundscape (rifle cracks, the headshot dink, shield breaks, footsteps, the storm's proximity rumble, the glider wind, the victory sting) is synthesized from oscillators and filtered noise with zero audio files; and the whole thing ships as a single hashed JavaScript bundle. The renderer runs a full post chain — FXAA antialiasing, restrained bloom, and a filmic grade pass with vignette, chromatic fringe and live grain — over an image-based-lit world: one prefiltered environment map gives every rig, gun and structure real reflections, aurora curtains and a moon hang in the procedural sky, and an adaptive governor steps the whole stack down before it ever lets the frame rate suffer. It is the same engineering practice as the rest of this domain — the Deep Signal lab descent shares its renderer discipline, adaptive quality stepping and synthesized audio approach — just pointed at a playable game instead of a guided one.

Your browser is showing the reading version. The arena needs a desktop-class browser with WebGL, a mouse and keyboard (pointer lock is how first-person aim works on the web), a window at least 960 pixels wide, and motion enabled. On a phone or tablet, or with reduced motion set, this page stays as the field manual you are reading now. The engineering notes land on the blog.
