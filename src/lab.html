[ LAB · 01 ]

Deep Signal

A descent through a growing crystal shaft — five chapters straight down, from the frozen surface to a beating core and back up into the light. This is the stackwith.me lab: one WebGL world, no page around it.

Unlike everything else on this domain, Deep Signal is built from authored, baked assets rather than procedural geometry. Six high-poly quartz-habit crystal variants — beveled hexagonal prisms carrying growth striations, stepped pyramidal terminations and parasite micro-crystals, several hundred to a few thousand triangles each — plus a matching low-detail LOD set and a dense bevelled hero gem are modelled headlessly in Blender, exported to glTF, and Draco-compressed to under forty kilobytes. The entire lighting model is a pair of 1024² Cycles-baked matcaps shipped as KTX2 textures: an exterior studio-ice map sampled by surface normal, and an interior refraction map sampled three times along chromatically dispersed rays, which is what gives every facet real internal light with spectral fringing at the edges. At runtime, ~1,600 instanced shards grow out of the shaft wall as you scroll past their birth depth — the same trick that makes igloo.inc's crystals feel alive — and only the depth bands near the camera are drawn at full detail; distant bands swap to the LOD set deep inside the fog.

The world also answers back: sweeping the pointer strikes the shaft wall and nearby crystals ring — a swell, a tinted glow, a pitched chime panned to where you touched — and inside THE CORE the pointer becomes an instrument: hold to charge the heart, release to send one shockwave through the whole field. The rest of the instrument panel is shared with the About journey: a keyframed camera on a Catmull-Rom rail, in-world SDF typography, a filmic master grade with per-chapter color, a synthesized soundscape behind the SND toggle, and an adaptive quality governor that trades resolution for frame time on whatever machine is in front of it.

Your browser is showing the reading version — the full descent needs WebGL, motion, and a viewport wider than 680px. The engineering notes behind this build land on the blog.
